# Evaluation Framework

## Purpose

The evaluation framework is the part of Idea Factory that answers a hard question plainly:

**Is this idea worth more of the user's time?**

This framework exists to prevent the system from becoming a concept-polishing machine. An idea can be clear, well-structured, and well-written, and still not deserve more effort. The evaluation layer is meant to surface that reality early.

The goal is not to produce a universal truth about the idea. The goal is to produce a structured, honest judgment based on what is currently known.

## Evaluation and judgment

Idea Factory separates two related layers.

### Evaluation

Evaluation is the analytical layer. It asks:

- what is strong,
- what is weak,
- what is assumed,
- what is risky,
- and what remains unclear.

### Judgment

Judgment is the decision layer. It asks:

- given the current evaluation,
- what should happen next?

In Phase I, both layers live together inside the **Evaluate** stage and the **Evaluation** page, but the distinction still matters.

## The eight criteria

The framework uses eight criteria.

### 1. Clarity

Can the concept be stated clearly enough to judge at all?

This includes:

- concept definition,
- audience definition,
- problem definition,
- mechanism understanding,
- and scope understanding.

If clarity is weak, later judgment must remain limited.

### 2. Coherence

Does the idea hold together internally?

This asks whether:

- the parts fit together,
- the mechanism supports the intended value,
- the scope aligns with the framing,
- and major contradictions remain unresolved.

An idea can be coherent and still weak. Coherence is about internal fit, not desirability.

### 3. Value

Does the idea appear to solve a meaningful problem or create a meaningful benefit?

This criterion asks:

- who benefits,
- whether the problem actually matters,
- whether the gain is concrete enough,
- and whether the value is more than abstract aspiration.

### 4. Differentiation

Does the idea appear meaningfully distinct from obvious defaults or familiar patterns?

This is not about novelty for its own sake. It is about whether the concept is actually saying something different enough to matter.

### 5. Plausibility

Does the idea seem workable enough, at the concept level, to justify further thought?

This is not full technical feasibility. It is a judgment about whether the mechanism and claimed outcome seem believable enough in their current form.

### 6. Defensibility

How easy is the idea to replicate, and how durable is any likely advantage?

This criterion is especially important for product concepts, but it can matter in other concept types as well.

It asks:

- if this works, how easy is it to copy,
- what would make the advantage durable,
- whether the concept depends only on the idea or also on execution, trust, access, credibility, or adoption,
- and whether the likely upside is protected enough to justify meaningful effort.

Weak defensibility should appear both as a criterion weakness and as a risk.

### 7. Effort-worthiness

Does the current form of the idea justify more time, focus, or money?

This is the most practical criterion.

An idea may be interesting but still not be worth continued effort. Effort-worthiness is where the system asks whether the likely upside, adjusted for weakness and uncertainty, actually justifies continued work.

### 8. Evidence integrity

How much of the judgment is grounded, and how much is inferred?

This criterion keeps the system honest about what it really knows.

It asks:

- whether the judgment is internal or externally validated,
- whether key claims rely on missing evidence,
- and how much uncertainty remains in the current recommendation.

## Internal and external judgment

This distinction is essential.

### Internal judgment

Internal judgment is based on:

- user input,
- the current concept definition,
- the current artifact,
- and the system's own reasoning.

This is the default in Phase I.

### External judgment

External judgment depends on outside research such as:

- competitor review,
- existing frameworks,
- comparable policy proposals,
- literature,
- or market scans.

Phase I may support this later, but it should never pretend that internal judgment is the same as externally validated judgment.

## Scale and scoring

The framework may use an internal numeric score for each criterion, but the visible interface should not present raw numbers as the primary truth.

The user should see a verbal scale such as:

- **Very weak**
- **Weak**
- **Fair**
- **Strong**
- **Very strong**
- **Not enough information**

For every criterion, the user should also see:

- a short reasoning statement,
- and an uncertainty note when needed.

This preserves comparability without creating false precision.

## Hard blockers and soft weaknesses

The framework should distinguish between two kinds of problems.

### Hard blockers

These prevent a positive recommendation in the current state.

Examples include:

- concept too unclear to judge,
- major contradiction,
- no meaningful value case,
- mechanism undefined in a blocking way,
- or harmful purpose.

### Soft weaknesses

These weaken the recommendation but do not automatically block continued work.

Examples include:

- weak differentiation,
- incomplete examples,
- moderate defensibility concerns,
- or some unresolved assumptions.

This distinction keeps the system from overreacting to ordinary weakness while still protecting against serious flaws.

## What the evaluation page should contain

The standard **Evaluation** page should include:

1. an overall assessment,
2. a criterion-by-criterion review,
3. open risks,
4. assumptions,
5. a recommendation,
6. and an uncertainty note.

This keeps the evaluation neutral, explicit, and legible.

## What the framework should never do

The evaluation framework should never:

- pretend certainty it does not have,
- convert polish into merit,
- confuse novelty with value,
- hide moral or legal concerns inside neutral language,
- or let scoring override recommendation logic.

## Summary

The Idea Factory evaluation framework exists to convert a concept from something merely described into something seriously judged.

It uses eight criteria:

- clarity,
- coherence,
- value,
- differentiation,
- plausibility,
- defensibility,
- effort-worthiness,
- and evidence integrity.

Its purpose is not to flatter the user or to produce a final truth. Its purpose is to make the next decision about the idea more honest.
